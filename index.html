„Put some colour to your life.“

One day Phaeton, the son of Helios and the God of the Sun, managed to convince his father to allow him to drive his horse-drawn chariot down the firmament. His father agreed, but as soon as the horses felt that the charioteer was inexperienced, they bolted. The sun burnt the African land to ashes, tanning its inhabitants black. So as to prevent further damage, Zeus was forced to strike Phaeton with a lightning bolt into the river Eridanus

Phaeton's sister Heliades, turned hm into poplar trees as a punishment. Grieving, they kept crying, and their tears became resin which turned into amber. Years later, the sea is still tossing the sister's amber tears to the shore.

The existence of several thousand-year old amber amulets is evidence that people believed in the power of the stones since the earliest prehistoric times. Amber has also been used as a kind of foundation stone, to ensure health and good luck for inhabitants of dwellings. This faith in the properties and effectiveness of amber continues to this day.

The uniqueness of amber also results from the visual properties of the raw stone itself. Forms create by nature are interesting, drops, icicles and nuggets with a natural opening used to be used as the first amulets. They were hung on a leather thong for protection or decoration.

As time goes by, amber is still perceived as an exceptional stone, due to it:

  • Smelling nice when warmed in your hand,
  • Giving out a resinous scent,
  • Aromatic smoke when burnt,
  • Statically charges and attracts small pieces of paper when rubbed,
  • Sinks in fresh water, but floats on saltwater,
  • Contains inclusions (traces of life dating back 40 million years).

Then there were figurines of animals, birds and fish, these were to guarantee successful hunting or fishing. People believed that in the same way that amber attracts dry glass blades, such amulets attract luck and happiness and have special powers.
